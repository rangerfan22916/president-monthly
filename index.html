John Quincy Adams

6th President of the United States (1825-1829)

Biography

John Quincy Adams, the eldest son of President John Adams, was born in 1767. He was a diplomat, senator, and secretary of state before becoming the sixth president of the United States.

Adams played a significant role in shaping U.S. foreign policy, negotiating key treaties and formulating the Monroe Doctrine. After his presidency, he served in the House of Representatives for 17 years, where he became a leading opponent of slavery.

Major Achievements

  • Negotiated the Treaty of Ghent (1814) – Adams played a vital role in ending the War of 1812 between the U.S. and Britain.
  • Authored the Monroe Doctrine – Shaped U.S. foreign policy by warning European nations against interference in the Americas.
  • Fought Against Slavery – Successfully defended the Amistad captives, a landmark case in abolitionist history.
  • Promoted National Infrastructure – Advocated for roads, canals, and a national university.
  • Helped Establish the Smithsonian Institution – Used Smithson’s donation to create a national center for research and knowledge.
